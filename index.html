Background & Foundation

I started with a strong engineering and project management foundation. I studied Industrial Engineering at Bilkent University, which shaped how I think about systems, constraints, dependencies, and optimisation.

When I moved to Berlin, I chose a Master's in Project Management & Data Science — not to become a pure data scientist, but because I believed every role would need to be data-literate to be effective.

Casper — Discovering Analytics Engineering

Casper was a defining moment. It was my first exposure to the modern data stack: AWS (S3 + Redshift), dbt, and Looker. I owned the Marketing Data Mart, standardising data from multiple channels and building a semantic layer in Looker that became the single source of truth.

This work went beyond dashboards — it involved defining what metrics mean, how they should be calculated, and how they should be consistently interpreted. Casper was one of the earliest dbt adopters, and this let me deeply internalise what analytics engineering really is.

Tier Mobility — Scale, Pressure, and Accountability

Tier was hyper-growth: massive data volume, small team, extreme pace. The company generated large-scale IoT event data and billing systems producing over 1 million invoices per month at peak.

I naturally became involved with the CFO and finance leadership, audit preparation, and investor reporting for funding rounds. This taught me how critical analytics engineering becomes when trust, money, and external scrutiny are involved.

Contentful — Maturity, Influence, and Structure

At Contentful, progress required influence, persuasion, and patience rather than speed. I focused on refactoring core ARR models, setting up end-to-end month-end closing, and founding a Data Steering Committee aligning Finance, Sales, Product, and Marketing.

This role matured me significantly in organisational impact — how to introduce governance and architectural improvements in environments where change isn't naturally welcomed.

Overall Perspective

Across all roles, a consistent theme: turning raw data into trusted analytical truth, designing systems that balance speed, correctness, and trust, and operating at the intersection of engineering, analytics, and business.

As AI starts querying data directly, the importance of well-defined semantics, stable models, and clear ownership only increases. My motivation is to build platforms that people and machines can trust.

How To Develop A Profitable App

12 Jan 2020

The goal of this project was to analyse data from the App Store and Google Play Store to understand what types of apps are likely to attract the most users — because in these markets, revenue is primarily driven by in-app ads, meaning more users equals more revenue.

The dataset included information on app categories, ratings, reviews, size, pricing, and installs across both platforms. I focused on free apps, since those are the ones that rely on ad-driven revenue models.

Key findings:

  • Apps in the Books and Reference, Communication, and Social categories showed the highest average install counts across both stores.
  • The Google Play Store had significantly more apps and installs than the App Store, making it a better starting platform for reaching a wide audience.
  • Many categories appeared saturated with low-quality apps — an opportunity for well-built alternatives that deliver real value.
  • Games had the highest volume of apps, but also the most competition and lowest average rating.

The recommendation: build a free app in an underserved but high-demand category (like books/reference), launch first on Google Play to maximise reach, then expand to the App Store once validated.

The analysis was done in Python using pandas, and the full notebook is available on my GitHub.

Collatz sequence (or Hailstone sequence)

18 Jun 2019

The Collatz conjecture is one of those beautifully simple unsolved problems in mathematics. Take any positive integer n. If it's even, divide it by 2. If it's odd, multiply by 3 and add 1. Repeat. The conjecture says you'll always eventually reach 1.

The sequence gets its nickname "Hailstone" because the numbers rise and fall unpredictably, like hailstones in a cloud, before eventually settling down.

The rules:

if n is even  ->  n / 2
if n is odd   ->  3n + 1
repeat until n = 1

Example: Starting with 6:

6 -> 3 -> 10 -> 5 -> 16 -> 8 -> 4 -> 2 -> 1

I wrote a simple Python function to generate and visualise these sequences. What's interesting is how wildly different starting numbers can behave — some reach 1 quickly, others take hundreds of steps, rocketing up to enormous values before crashing back down.

def collatz(n):
    sequence = [n]
    while n != 1:
        if n % 2 == 0:
            n = n // 2
        else:
            n = 3 * n + 1
        sequence.append(n)
    return sequence

Despite being so easy to state and implement, no one has been able to prove the Collatz conjecture holds for all numbers. Paul Erdos famously said: "Mathematics is not yet ready for such problems."
